01.

About

I'm a computer science student from India who believes the best software is the kind that stops someone from getting hurt — a pothole that never got reported, an accident that never had to happen.

I competed in Smart India Hackathon 2025, where my team built SnapSamasya — a full-stack civic safety platform with two sides: a citizen portal where anyone can photograph and report potholes, track complaint status in real time, and receive notifications when action is taken; and a municipal dashboard where authorities assign work orders to field teams, monitor resolution progress, and close verified complaints. The mission: prevent the accidents, injuries, and vehicle damage caused by unrepaired road hazards every single day.

I'm currently building a strong foundation in machine learning and deep learning to explore research at the intersection of civic technology and AI. At this stage, I am studying research papers and creating summaries to better understand the field. My goal for 2026 is to contribute meaningfully to open source, secure an internship in ML/CV, and publish at least one research paper.

Outside of research, I build web interfaces with obsessive attention to detail, write Python tools for fun, and read broadly — philosophy of science, systems thinking, anything that sharpens how I frame problems.

Smart India Hackathon 2025

India's largest national-level hackathon. Problem statement: build a system to reduce road accident risk from unrepaired potholes and civic infrastructure failure.

Built SnapSamasya — a full-stack civic safety platform with two distinct sides. The citizen portal lets anyone photograph a pothole, submit a geotagged complaint, and track its status in real time — from "reported" through "assigned" to "resolved." The municipal dashboard gives local authorities a live feed of incoming complaints, tools to assign work orders to specific field teams, and a workflow to mark repairs as complete. The YOLOv8 model running underneath auto-detects and classifies road damage from uploaded footage at 94.2% confidence. The whole system went from zero to working demo in 36 hours.

Lessons from the Field

1.
Scope ruthlessly. A working demo of one feature beats a broken demo of ten. Hackathons force you to identify the single most defensible core.
2.
Communicate the "so what." Judges are rarely engineers. The clearest path from model accuracy to citizen impact wins every time.
3.
Real impact comes from closing the loop. SnapSamasya only works because both sides — citizens and municipalities — are in the same system. A complaint that goes nowhere is worse than no system at all. The research question that followed: how do you build trust into civic technology so people actually use it?
