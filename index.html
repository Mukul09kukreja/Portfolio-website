02.

Research

My research sits at the intersection of computer vision, deep learning, and civic infrastructure — asking how intelligent systems can reduce friction between citizens and public services.

Working Paper P — 01 2025–2026

Automated Road Damage Detection and Civic Reporting Using YOLOv8 and Real-Time Computer Vision

Mukul Kukreja, et al. · B.Tech CS, India · Targeting IEEE / ACM
Abstract

This paper presents a lightweight, deployable system for real-time detection of road surface damage — potholes, cracks, and structural hazards — using the YOLOv8 object detection architecture. The system integrates GPS telemetry, image geolocation, and automated API calls to government grievance portals, filing civic complaints without human intervention. Evaluated on NH-48 corridor footage, the model achieves 94.2% detection confidence at 24 fps on consumer hardware. We discuss deployment constraints in bandwidth-limited environments and propose a citizen-facing mobile extension for crowdsourced data collection.

Lessons from the Field

1.
Scope ruthlessly. A working demo of one feature beats a broken demo of ten. Hackathons force you to identify the single most defensible core.
2.
Communicate the "so what." Judges are rarely engineers. The clearest path from model accuracy to citizen impact wins every time.
3.
Research emerges under pressure. SnapSamasya began as a 36-hour prototype. The questions it raised — bandwidth constraints, data bias, edge deployment — became my research agenda.
